The Navkar Mantra, word by word
णमोकार मंत्र का अर्थ
›
LineMeaning
णमो अरिहंताणंI bow to the Arihants — souls who destroyed the four inner enemies (anger, ego, deceit, greed) and attained omniscience, but still live in a body and teach the path. The Tirthankaras are Arihants.
णमो सिद्धाणंI bow to the Siddhas — fully liberated souls who have shed all karma and dwell forever at the top of the universe (Siddhashila) in infinite bliss.
णमो आयरियाणंI bow to the Acharyas — the heads of the monastic order who lead the sangh and protect the conduct of dharma.
णमो उवज्झायाणंI bow to the Upadhyays — the teacher-monks who study and teach the scriptures.
णमो लोए सव्व साहूणंI bow to all Sadhus in the world — every monk and nun anywhere who walks the path of restraint.
एसो पंच णमोक्कारोThis five-fold salutation…
सव्व पावप्पणासणो…destroys all sins…
मंगलाणं च सव्वेसिं…and of all auspicious things…
पढमं हवई मंगलं…it is the foremost auspicious one.
Notice: the Navkar bows to qualities, not to any individual by name. Anyone who attains those qualities is included — that is why it is called the universal mantra.
The Three Jewels (Ratnatraya)
रत्नत्रय — मोक्ष का मार्ग
›

The Tattvartha Sutra opens: "Samyag darshan gyan charitrani moksha margah" — right faith, right knowledge and right conduct together are the path to liberation.

Samyak Darshanसम्यक् दर्शन — Right faith: seeing reality as it is; conviction in the true nature of soul and karma.
Samyak Gyanसम्यक् ज्ञान — Right knowledge: understanding the truths free of doubt and distortion.
Samyak Charitraसम्यक् चारित्र — Right conduct: living that knowledge through the vows and restraints.

All three are needed together — like a medicine you must trust, understand, and actually take.

The Five Great Vows (Mahavrats)
पाँच महाव्रत
›
1. Ahimsaअहिंसा — Non-violence toward every living being, in thought, word and deed. The supreme dharma: "Ahimsa Paramo Dharmah."
2. Satyaसत्य — Truthfulness; speaking only what is true, beneficial and kind.
3. Asteyaअस्तेय — Non-stealing; taking nothing that is not freely given.
4. Brahmacharyaब्रह्मचर्य — Celibacy for monks; fidelity and restraint for householders.
5. Aparigrahaअपरिग्रह — Non-possessiveness; limiting attachment to possessions, people and opinions.

Monks follow these absolutely (mahavrats); householders follow gentler versions called anuvrats (small vows), extended by 3 gunavrats and 4 shikshavrats into the 12 vows of a shravak.

The Twelve Bhavnas (Reflections)
बारह भावना
›

Twelve contemplations that loosen attachment and steady the soul — the theme of the famous "Barah Bhavana" stavan:

  1. Anitya — everything is impermanent
  2. Asharan — nothing worldly can shelter the soul from karma
  3. Sansar — the cycle of birth and death is endless wandering
  4. Ekatva — the soul comes alone and goes alone
  5. Anyatva — the soul is separate even from the body
  6. Ashuchi — the body is impure; the soul within is the jewel
  7. Asrava — passions let karma flow in
  8. Samvar — restraint stops the inflow
  9. Nirjara — austerity sheds karma already bound
  10. Lok — contemplate the vastness of the universe
  11. Bodhi-durlabh — right insight is rare and precious
  12. Dharma — true dharma is the supreme refuge
Karma — the eight types
आठ कर्म
›

In Jainism karma is not fate — it is fine matter that sticks to the soul through passions. Eight kinds bind the soul:

Ghati (destructive) karmas — block the soul's own qualities

GyanavarniyaVeils knowledge
DarshanavarniyaVeils perception
MohaniyaDeludes — the hardest to break; source of attachment and aversion
AntarayObstructs energy, charity, enjoyment

Aghati (non-destructive) karmas — shape the embodiment

VedaniyaBrings pleasure and pain
AyushyaFixes the lifespan
NaamShapes the body and its features
GotraDetermines status of birth

Destroying the four ghati karmas makes a soul an Arihant (omniscient). When the remaining four fall away at death, the soul becomes a Siddha.

The Fourteen Gunasthans (stages of the soul)
चौदह गुणस्थान
›

The ladder every soul climbs from delusion to liberation — simplified:

  1. Mithyatva — wrong belief (where most souls are)
  2. Sasvadan — a falling soul's brief aftertaste of right faith
  3. Mishra — mixed belief and doubt
  4. Avirat Samyagdrishti — right faith gained, but no vows yet
  5. Deshvirati — partial vows: the stage of a practicing shravak
  6. Pramatta Sanyat — full monk's vows, with some carelessness
  7. Apramatta Sanyat — vigilant restraint
  8. –12. The subtle ladder where passions are suppressed or destroyed
  9. Sayogi Kevali — omniscient with a body: the Arihant
  10. Ayogi Kevali — the final instant before moksha
Samayik briefly lifts a householder near the 6th–7th stage — which is why 48 minutes of equanimity is considered so precious.
The Six Daily Essentials (Avashyaks)
छह आवश्यक
›

Six practices every Jain is meant to do daily — Pratikraman weaves all six together:

  1. Samayik — 48 minutes of equanimity
  2. Chauvisattho — praising the 24 Tirthankaras (the Logassa sutra)
  3. Vandan — venerating the Guru
  4. Pratikraman — turning back from the day's faults
  5. Kayotsarg — standing meditation, letting go of the body
  6. Pachkhan — taking a vow of renunciation for the day ahead
Jain answers to big questions
तत्त्व ज्ञान
›

Is there a creator God?

No — the universe is eternal and runs on its own laws. Jains worship the Tirthankaras not as creators but as perfected souls showing the way. Every soul has the same potential: "Appa so Paramappa" — the soul itself is the supreme soul.

What is the soul?

Every living being — human, animal, insect, plant, even microscopic nigod — has an individual, eternal soul (jiva) whose true nature is infinite knowledge, perception, bliss and energy. Karma covers it like clouds cover the sun.

Why ahimsa above all?

Because every soul feels pain as we do, and harming any being binds heavy karma to the harmer. Compassion is not just kindness — it is self-purification.

What is Anekantavad?

The doctrine of many-sidedness: truth has many facets, and no single viewpoint captures all of it (like the blind men and the elephant). This is Jainism's gift of intellectual humility and tolerance.
